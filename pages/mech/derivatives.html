Derivatives

A derivative of a function \(f:x\mapsto y\) gives a linear approximation of how a perturbation in \(\Delta x\) translates into a pertubation in \(\Delta y\). The derivative notation was in fact designed by Leibniz to ellicit this idea of ratios of perturbations $$ \Delta y = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \Delta x $$ Remembering this form can be quite useful in taking derivatives. The other critical piece is the product rule, ie. if the function has multiple arguments perturb each of them separately and sum. Vector Derivatives

For linear function \(f:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), \(f(x) = c^Tx\) $$ \Delta y = c^T\Delta x \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = c^T $$

For linear function \(f:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^m\), \(f(x) = Ax\) $$ \Delta y = A\Delta x \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = A $$

For quadratic functions \(f:\mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), \(f(x) = x^TQx\) $$ \Delta y =\Delta x^T Q x + x^TQ\Delta x = (\Delta x^T Q x)^T + x^TQ\Delta x = x^T (Q^T + Q) \Delta x $$ Note that transposing scalar values can often be useful in calculations. Since \(\tfrac{1}{2}(Q+Q^T)\) is the symmetric component of \(Q\), we always assume \(Q\) is symmetric and thus we often write $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = x^T(Q+Q^T) = 2x^T(Q) $$
