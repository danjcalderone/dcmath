Rotation Matrix
SYMMETRIC MATRIX

The inverse of a rotation matrix is its transpose $$ R^{-1} = R^T $$ This relationship is equivalent to the columns being orthonormal. Explicitly $$ R^{-1}R = R^TR =I $$ $$ R^TR = \begin{bmatrix} - & R_1^T & - \\ & \vdots & \\ - & R_n^T & - \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} | & & | \\ R_1 & \cdots & R_n \\ | & & | \end{bmatrix} $$ $$ \begin{bmatrix} R_1^TR_1 & \cdots & R_1^TR_n \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ R_n^TR_1 & \cdots & R_n^TR_n \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$ Elementwise: $$ R_i^TR_j = \begin{cases} 1 & i = j \textbf{ (normal)} \\ 0 & i \neq j \textbf{ (orthogonal)} \end{cases} $$ Visually this means the columns (and rows) of \(R\) are orthogonal vectors on the unit sphere.

Example: a counter-clockwise 2D rotation given by $$ R_{2 \times 2} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos \theta & - \sin \theta \\ \sin \theta & \cos \theta \end{bmatrix} $$

Planes and Angles of Rotation

The eigenvalues of a rotation matrix take the form $$ \lambda_{k,k+} = \cos \theta_k \pm i \sin \theta_k $$ For dimensions greater than \(2 \times 2\), there may be multiple angles \(\theta_k\)

A rotation matrix can be decomposed into multiple individual planes (and angles) of rotation. This "semi-diagonalization" can be written as $$ R = UDU^T $$ $$ R = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & & | & | & | \\ | & | & & | & | & | \\ U_1 & V_1 & \cdots & U_k & V_k & \sqrt{2}u \\ | & | & & | & | & | \\ | & | & & | & | & | \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c\theta_1 & -s\theta_1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ s\theta_1 & c\theta_1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & c\theta_k & -s\theta_k & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & s\theta_k & c\theta_k & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} - & - & U_1^T & - & -\\ - & - & V_1^T & - & -\\ & & \vdots & & \\ - & - & U_k^T & - & -\\ - & - & V_k^T & - & -\\ - & - & \sqrt{2}u^T & - & - \end{bmatrix} $$ The last column, \(u\) is an axis of rotation which most exist for odd dimensional matrices since the eigenvalues must come in conjugate pairs. Note that if some of the angles \(\theta_j = 0\) then the "axis" of rotation may actually be a higher-dimensional subspace. For even dimensional matrices there may be no axis of rotation

The columns of \(U\) are orthonormal, ie. \(U^TU =I\). Each pair \(U_j,V_j\) form an orthonormal basis for a plane of rotation and are not unique within that plane (any orthonormal basis for that plane will do). The matrix \(R\) rotates by an angle of \(\theta_j\) within that plane. Each plane of rotation is orthonormal to the others (and any axes of rotation). In 2D, there is just one plane of rotation. In 3D, there is one plane of rotation and an axis of rotation. In 4D, there are two a separate planes of rotation. In 5D, there are two planes and one axis, etc.

Planes and Angles of Rotation

The above "semi"-diagonalization is related to the full diagonalization (with complex eigenvalues and eigenvectors) through the relationship $$ \begin{bmatrix} c\theta & s\theta \\ s \theta & c\theta \end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ -i & i \end{bmatrix} }_{W} \begin{bmatrix} c\theta+is\theta & 0 \\ 0 & c\theta-is\theta \end{bmatrix} \underbrace{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & i\\ 1 & -i \end{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}}_{W^*} $$ Note that this is the diagonalization of a 2D rotation matrix and that \(W\) is unitary, ie. \(W^*W =I\). Plugging in, we can compute the full diagonalization. $$ R = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} | & | & & | & | & | \\ | & | & & | & | & | \\ U_1-iV_1 & U_1+V_1i & \cdots & U_k-V_ki & U_k+V_ki & u \\ | & | & & | & | & | \\ | & | & & | & | & | \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c\theta_1 & -s\theta_1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ s\theta_1 & c\theta_1 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & c\theta_k & -s\theta_k & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & s\theta_k & c\theta_k & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} - & - & U_1^T+V_1^Ti & - & -\\ - & - & U_1^T - V_1^Ti & - & -\\ & & \vdots & & \\ - & - & U_k^T+V_k^Ti & - & -\\ - & - & U_k^T-V_k^Ti & - & -\\ - & - & u^T & - & - \end{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} $$ Note that \(V\) is unitary, ie. \(V^*V = I\). \(U\) and \(V\) are relate via \(W\) and we can show that \(V\) is unitary as follows $$ V = U \underbrace{ \begin{bmatrix} W & \cdots & 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & W & 0 \\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} }_{\tilde{W}} \qquad \qquad \qquad V^*V = U^T\tilde{W}^*\tilde{W}U = U^TIU = I $$
